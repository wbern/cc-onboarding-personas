# Persona: The Strategist

**Internal ID**: `strategist`
**Created**: 2026-04-11
**Status**: Draft

## Display Name

**The Strategist**

Why not "Optimizer" or "Efficiency Seeker": while accurate, those names center on cost
avoidance. "Strategist" reframes the behavior as intentional, intelligent resource
allocation — which is how this persona sees themselves. It's aspirational without being
dishonest. Alternatives considered: The Pragmatist (too dry), The Resourceful (awkward
as a noun), The Optimizer (slightly transactional).

## Tagline

"Finds the smartest path to every result"

## Card Bullets

- Researches every option before committing
- Finds creative ways to get more from less
- Tracks ROI on tools like a portfolio
- Upgrades only when the math checks out

## Illustration Direction

Minimalistic geometric figure studying a chessboard or holding a magnifying glass over a
toolbox. Same silhouette style as the Builder (pawn-like form), but posed in a
deliberate, analytical stance. Conveys calculation, not hesitation.

---

## Key Traits

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Resourceful** | Finds workarounds, free tiers, open-source alternatives, and community solutions before reaching for a credit card. |
| **ROI-driven** | Every tool must justify its cost in time saved or output gained. Tracks value instinctively. |
| **Research-first** | Reads comparisons, community threads, and changelogs before signing up. Rarely impulse-adopts. |
| **Self-sufficient** | Prefers documentation and self-serve over sales calls. Wants to evaluate on their own terms. |
| **Pragmatic** | Not anti-spending — anti-waste. Will pay gladly when the value is proven and the price is fair. |

## Motivations

- **Maximize output per dollar**: AI is a force multiplier, and they want the highest
  leverage at the lowest cost. Not stingy — strategic.
- **Stay ahead without overcommitting**: Wants to integrate AI into daily workflow before
  competitors do, but won't bet the budget on unproven tools.
- **Maintain autonomy**: Distrusts vendor lock-in. Prefers tools that work alongside
  their existing stack rather than replacing it.
- **Look smart to their team**: Bringing in a powerful tool at zero or low cost earns
  credibility. Being the person who "found the free way" is a point of pride.

## Pain Points

- **Opaque pricing**: Hidden costs, usage-based billing surprises, and "contact sales"
  walls are immediate deal-breakers. They want to calculate cost before they start.
- **Free tiers that feel like traps**: Generous enough to hook you, restrictive enough
  to force an upgrade before you've proven value. They've been burned before.
- **Time wasted on setup**: If the free experience requires 30 minutes of config before
  showing value, they'll move on. Time is their most scarce resource.
- **Feature gating on essentials**: Paywalling core functionality (not premium extras)
  feels adversarial. They respect limits on scale, not on capability.
- **No clear upgrade path**: They want to see exactly what paid unlocks and whether it
  maps to their specific bottleneck. Generic "upgrade for more" messaging doesn't work.

## Attitude Toward Pricing & Free Tiers

This persona is the most sophisticated free-tier user in any SaaS product. They will:

- **Map the free tier completely** before considering paid. They know every limit,
  every workaround, every creative combination of free tools that approximates
  paid features.
- **Compare aggressively**: Claude Code vs. Cursor vs. Copilot vs. open-source
  alternatives (Aider, Continue). They maintain mental (or actual) spreadsheets.
- **Respect fair pricing**: They're not looking for everything free forever. They
  understand that sustainable tools need revenue. What they resent is feeling
  manipulated into paying before they've proven value to themselves.
- **Convert in bursts**: When they hit a genuine ceiling — a project deadline, a
  task that clearly needs the paid tier — they'll upgrade without hesitation.
  The conversion is sudden, not gradual.

**Industry context**: Freemium SaaS typically converts 2-5% of free users to paid
(source: ProfitWell, a16z). But strategist-type users convert at higher rates when
the upgrade trigger is a natural ceiling ("I've outgrown this") rather than an
artificial paywall. Companies like Notion, Figma, and Slack have found that lowering
the entry price and removing seat minimums can quadruple conversion rates for this
segment, even at lower ACV.

## What Converts Them to Paid

Ranked by effectiveness:

1. **Hitting a real ceiling during real work**: They're mid-project, the free tier
   runs out (rate limits, context length, usage caps), and the upgrade is one click
   away. This is the #1 converter. The limit must feel like a consequence of
   success, not a punishment.

2. **Transparent ROI math**: Show them: "You used Claude Code 47 times this week.
   At $X/month, that's $Y per use." Let them do the math themselves — they will,
   and they'll convert if it checks out.

3. **Time-limited full access**: A 7-day trial of the full product (not a crippled
   "premium trial") lets them build the habit. Once the workflow is integrated,
   removing it creates real friction.

4. **Team/social proof**: "Your team already has 3 paid seats" or "Used by 50K
   developers this week" works because it validates their judgment, not just the tool.

5. **Exclusive capability, not more of the same**: Features that unlock a
   qualitatively different workflow (not just "more tokens") — e.g., multi-file
   editing, project-wide refactoring, advanced integrations.

What does NOT convert them:
- Countdown timers or urgency tactics (they see through these instantly)
- "Upgrade to unlock" modals on basic features (feels hostile)
- Sales outreach before they've asked for it (privacy violation in their eyes)

## Ideal Onboarding Tone

**Core principle**: Show value fast, respect their time.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Get to a working demo in under 2 minutes | Require account creation before showing value |
| Show the install command immediately | Hide the product behind a marketing funnel |
| Let them copy-paste and see results | Force a guided tour they can't skip |
| Acknowledge they're evaluating options | Assume they've already decided |
| Be transparent about what's free vs. paid | Obscure limits until they hit them |
| Provide a "skip ahead" path for experienced users | Force linear progression through basics they already know |

**Voice**: Direct, efficient, lightly technical. Avoid marketing superlatives
("revolutionary", "game-changing"). Use concrete claims ("saves ~20 min per PR
review"). This persona responds to evidence, not enthusiasm.

**Pacing**: The Builder wants to tinker and explore. The Strategist wants to
evaluate and decide. Onboarding for this persona should front-load the value
proposition: what does Claude Code do, how fast can I try it, what will it cost
me if I like it.

## What Success Looks Like

For the Strategist, onboarding succeeds when:

1. **They get Claude Code running in under 5 minutes** and see it do something
   useful (not a hello-world — an actual task they'd do at work).
2. **They understand the free tier boundaries** before they hit them. No surprises.
3. **They bookmark the site or save the install command** — a signal that they
   intend to return, even if they don't convert today.
4. **They tell a colleague**: "I found this thing, it's actually good, and you
   can start for free." Word-of-mouth from Strategists is high-quality because
   their recommendation carries implicit due-diligence.

**Failure state**: They bounce at the platform check (no Windows/Linux support),
feel the onboarding is too basic for their skill level, or can't find pricing
information and assume the worst.

## Freemium SaaS Patterns for This Persona

Research into how successful freemium products handle strategist-type users:

- **Slack**: Free tier is genuinely useful (10K message history, integrations).
  Conversion happens when teams outgrow the archive limit — a natural ceiling.
  Strategists convert when institutional knowledge starts disappearing.

- **Notion**: Generous free tier for individuals. Converts on collaboration
  (team workspaces) and scale (file upload limits). Strategists use it solo
  for months before bringing in their team — then convert on the team plan.

- **Figma**: Full editor access for free (3 projects). Converts on project
  count and team libraries. Strategists hit the 3-project wall mid-quarter
  and upgrade because the switching cost is already too high.

- **GitHub Copilot / Cursor**: Free tier with usage caps. Converts when the
  AI assistance becomes part of the muscle memory — removing it feels like
  losing a limb. Strategists track their productivity delta and convert
  when the data supports it.

**Common pattern**: The products that convert Strategists best are those where
the free tier creates genuine dependency through daily use, and the upgrade
trigger is a natural growth moment, not an artificial restriction. The ceiling
should feel like a congratulations ("you're using this enough to need more"),
not a roadblock.
